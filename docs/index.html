Knowledge graphs like Wikidata are full of holes — a shrine with no recorded location, no founding date, no enshrined deity. Large language models know a lot of these facts. This project measures, for one bounded and well-curated domain (Shinto shrines), how reliably an LLM can fill those gaps, and whether asking the model to verify its own answer turns guesses into trustworthy suggestions.

Research question How reliably can a large language model fill in missing factual statements in Wikidata for a bounded domain — Shinto shrines — and does an explicit self-verification pass meaningfully reduce the rate of wrong / hallucinated values? Measured as precision / recall by property type, predict-only vs predict-then-verify.

What this project does

1 · The question

Can an LLM fill missing Wikidata statements for Shinto shrines at usable precision, and how much does a self-verification pass help? Evaluated by holding out known values and measuring precision/recall per property type.

2 · Grounded in the literature

Prior work shows LLM factual recall collapses on torso/tail entities and varies sharply by property type (countries easy, dates hard), and that self-verification helps with factual QA but can also backfire. What's missing — and what o1 supplies — is the maintainer's decision table for one curated domain: precision/recall per property type, with vs without a verify pass. Full survey in literature/REVIEW.md.

3 · The finding (first bounded run)

Local Gemma fills country perfectly (1.00) and instance-of reasonably (0.67), but abstains on most dates and all coordinates. Overall predict-only precision was 0.50 / recall 0.33 on 42 held-out statements — and a self-verification pass lowered precision (0.50→0.43), matching the literature's warning that naive self-correction can backfire.
